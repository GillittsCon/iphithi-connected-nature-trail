# iPhithi hackathon content notes

## Stop 1 — Pollinators / Grassland
Title: Pollinators / Grassland
Hook line: Pollinators don’t stop at the fence.
Before image filename: pollinators-before.webp
Before image caption: iPhithi before restoration, showing bare, damaged ground after tree felling.
Transformation line: From invasive plantation slope to restored KwaZulu-Natal Sandstone Sourveld grassland.
What it supports now: dassies, birds, butterflies, insects, browsers (duiker/bushbuck), genets and more
Supporting image 1:
Supporting image 2:
Quick View: Grassland flowers and grasses feed bees, butterflies, moths, and other pollinators. These insects do not stop at the reserve fence — they move through nearby gardens too, helping plants flower, set seed, and support other wildlife.
Go Deeper: Go Deeper: Pollinators are a living link between the reserve and nearby gardens. They move from flower to flower across the suburb, so a healthy grassland can help support pollinator life beyond iPhithi’s boundaries. Diverse indigenous plants matter because they provide food and shelter across seasons, which helps pollinators, plant reproduction, and the wider food web.
Reserve → Suburb → Garden: A garden with few flowers is like an empty pantry. Even a small mix of indigenous blooms can become a stepping stone that extends the reserve’s pollinator network into the suburb.
Try this at home: Plant indigenous flowers in clusters, leave one messy corner with leaf litter or seed heads, or avoid pesticides that harm more than pests.

## Stop 2 — Water / Wetland
Title: Water / Wetland
Hook line: This wetland works harder than it looks.
Before image filename: wetland-before.webp
Before image caption: iPhithi before restoration, showing old tree stumps and dry ground before water returned.
Transformation line: From a dried-up stream to one of the most productive wetlands in Durban’s Outer West.
What it supports now: 3 mongoose species, other water life (crabs, frogs, etc), birds, provides drinking water for larger animals
Supporting image 1:
Supporting image 2:
Quick View: This wetland slows water moving through the landscape. That helps reduce runoff, trap sediment, and support cleaner water moving through the suburb downstream.
Go Deeper: Go Deeper: Wetlands are quiet infrastructure. They slow water, hold it in the landscape for longer, and help trap sediment and pollutants as it moves through. In a suburb, that means less sudden runoff, cleaner downstream water, and a landscape that copes better with both heavy rain and dry periods.
Reserve → Suburb → Garden: In built-up areas, hard surfaces push rainwater away fast. A functioning wetland helps slow that system down, but gardens can help too by letting more rain soak in where it falls.
Try this at home: Create a small soak zone with indigenous plants, use mulch to improve absorption, or keep natural drainage paths clear.

## Stop 3 — Green Corridors / Woodland
Title: Green Corridors / Woodland
Hook line: Wildlife needs a way through.
Before image filename: corridors-before.webp
Before image caption: iPhithi before restoration, showing dense plantation and bare ground as clearing began.
Transformation line: From dense plantation with little ground life to indigenous woodland that wildlife can move through.
What it supports now: Duiker, bushbuck, porcupines, genets, birds, bush pig, mongooses and more, use these protective corridors
Supporting image 1:
Supporting image 2:
Quick View: This woodland edge helps form a green corridor for wildlife. When animals can move between gardens, reserves, and other green spaces, they have a better chance of feeding, sheltering, and breeding.
Go Deeper: Go Deeper: Nature does not thrive in isolated islands. Green corridors help wildlife move between gardens, reserves, and other patches of habitat for feeding, shelter, breeding, and recovery after disturbance. In suburbs, connected planting, layered vegetation, and fewer barriers can make the difference between a broken landscape and a living network.
Reserve → Suburb → Garden: A reserve works better when it is not cut off. Gardens can either block movement or help create stepping stones that connect wildlife to shelter, food, and safer routes.
Try this at home: Add indigenous layering — even one shrub or small tree can help create a stepping stone between green spaces.
